\begin{abstract}
Diffusion auctions sell items through a social network: buyers not only bid, but also decide whether to forward the auction information. IDM resolves the single-item diffusion problem but is vulnerable to false-name manipulation; SCM restores Sybil-proofness in the single-item setting; and GIDM extends diffusion auctions to multiple homogeneous items without addressing Sybil attacks. This project studies the open direction at the intersection of these works. We propose \emph{SC-GIDM}, a multi-item cluster mechanism inspired by SCM and powered by GIDM on a cluster graph. The report has two goals. First, it explains the baseline mechanisms---IDM, SCM, and GIDM---using examples. Second, it formalizes SC-GIDM and proves two main results under a non-colluding type-preserving split-attack model: (i) SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof, because a buyer's fake identities stay inside the same cluster and cannot change the macro GIDM instance; and (ii) SC-GIDM inherits the relevant macro-level guarantees of GIDM, namely feasibility, individual rationality, incentive compatibility, and weak budget balance. We also justify the cluster-root reward rule as weakly incentive compatible under ex ante role uncertainty.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Classical auctions assume that the seller can directly contact all buyers. In many online and social-network markets this assumption fails: a seller first reaches only a small local neighborhood, and deeper buyers learn about the sale only if informed agents choose to propagate the information. This creates a new strategic tension. A buyer can help the seller by diffusing the auction, but may also prefer not to invite stronger competitors.

This line of work begins with the single-item diffusion model of Li et al., where buyers report both valuations and diffusion choices \cite{LiHZZ17}. Their information diffusion mechanism (IDM) is individually rational (IR), incentive compatible (IC), and weakly budget balanced, while a direct network-VCG extension can pay excessive rewards and may create seller deficit. Zhao et al.\ later extended the framework to multiple homogeneous items via the generalized information diffusion mechanism (GIDM), again preserving truthfulness and budget balance under unit-demand buyers \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}. However, Chen et al.\ showed that ordinary diffusion mechanisms are fragile to \emph{Sybil attacks}: a real buyer can create fake identities to manipulate the graph positions that determine rewards and payments. They proposed STM and SCM as single-item Sybil-proof mechanisms and explicitly left the multi-item case open \cite{ChenDWWZ23}.

Our project addresses this open direction. Since the final deliverable is not merely a short paper but also a report of the project, we do two things. We first explain the baseline mechanisms---IDM, SCM, and GIDM---and the source of their vulnerability or robustness. We then formalize our proposed extension, \emph{SC-GIDM}, and prove what it guarantees under an explicit attack model. Throughout, we keep a placeholder for the project implementation:

\subsection{Contributions}
Our report makes the following contributions.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item We explain the baseline single-item and multi-item diffusion mechanisms using small running examples, emphasizing where critical-path payments come from.
    \item We isolate and illustrate a Sybil attack on ordinary GIDM, showing how an attacker can increase its reward without adding real value.
    \item We formalize SC-GIDM: dominator clustering, cluster-graph pruning inspired by SCM, GIDM on the resulting tree, and a mixed item/reward rule in which winning clusters assign the item to the highest bidder while intermediary rewards go to cluster roots.
    \item We prove that SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof under a non-colluding type-preserving split-attack model and that it inherits the relevant macro-level guarantees of GIDM.
    \item We add a theorem showing that the root-based intermediary reward distribution is weakly incentive compatible under ex ante role uncertainty.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Related Work}
The first layer of the literature is the classical efficient-auction foundation due to Vickrey, Clarke, and Groves \cite{Vickrey61,Clarke71,Groves73}. The second layer adapts those ideas to network diffusion. Li et al.\ showed that network-VCG can make truthful diffusion compatible with efficiency, but may pay too much to critical intermediates and therefore run a deficit \cite{LiHZZ17}. IDM replaces ``reward all criticality'' by ``reward only price-improving diffusion,'' thus restoring non-deficit. Zhao et al.\ generalized this to $K$ homogeneous items with unit-demand buyers through GIDM, where a DFS-like routing and displacement process is coupled with counterfactual welfare payments \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}. The third layer studies robustness. Chen et al.\ showed that IDM-style criticality rewards are vulnerable to false-name manipulation and proposed STM and SCM for the single-item case \cite{ChenDWWZ23}. Our mechanism sits at the missing intersection of the last two papers: multi-item routing on one hand and Sybil robustness on the other.

\section{Background: IDM, SCM, and GIDM}
\subsection{What is a Sybil attack?}
A Sybil attack means that one real buyer creates one or more fake identities and controls all of them. In a diffusion auction this is especially dangerous because rewards depend on graph position. If fake identities can become apparently critical, the attacker may earn additional reward without contributing new real demand.

\subsection{IDM: single-item diffusion without deficit}
In the single-item setting, each informed buyer reports a valuation and chooses which neighbors to inform. The main difficulty is obvious: why would a buyer ever invite future competitors? Li et al.\ answered this through IDM \cite{LiHZZ17}. The mechanism first computes the diffusion critical sequence of the highest bidder and then allocates the item to the first buyer on that sequence who survives the relevant price test. The winner pays the highest bid obtainable without her, and each on-path buyer is rewarded only for the \emph{increase in price} generated by her diffusion. This is what makes IDM non-deficit.

The important point for our project is conceptual: IDM rewards \emph{graph-critical positions}. That is exactly why it is vulnerable to false-name manipulation. A fake node can change the critical sequence and thereby change the payment difference that creates the intermediary reward.

\subsection{SCM: single-item Sybil-proof clustering}
Chen et al.\ address this vulnerability in the single-item setting \cite{ChenDWWZ23}. Their Sybil Cluster Mechanism (SCM) first reconstructs a graph on trustworthy non-Sybil components, samples a shortest-path tree, deletes redundant inter-cluster edges, and then runs STM on the reduced graph. The purpose of this pruning is subtle but important: it can make some brokers uniquely critical again, thereby restoring positive reward opportunities while preserving Sybil-proofness. This idea directly inspires our own use of a pruned cluster graph.

\subsection{GIDM: multiple homogeneous items}
GIDM generalizes IDM to $K$ homogeneous items \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}. It does \emph{not} simply repeat IDM $K$ times. Instead, it computes an optimal allocation tree, routes items through the tree in a DFS-like manner, allows upstream nodes to displace downstream allocations, and then computes payments through welfare counterfactuals. The payment rule has the same general shape as in IDM: winners pay a counterfactual welfare loss, and intermediaries are rewarded according to the gap between two counterfactual welfare terms.

The full GIDM pseudocode is fairly long, so we place it in Appendix~\ref{app:gidm}. SC-GIDM uses that GIDM allocation/payment logic on a cluster graph rather than on the original reported identity graph.

\section{GIDM Sybil Vulnerability}
We now isolate the specific reason why ordinary GIDM is vulnerable. In the truthful instance (\ref{fig:gidmattack}), buyer $C$ is an intermediary and receives reward $1$ (equivalently payment $-1$). In the attacked instance (\ref{fig:gidmattack}), the same buyer creates a fake child $C'$ with bid $18.9$. This Sybil does not add new real value, but it changes the counterfactual welfare benchmark used in GIDM's payment formula. As a result, the attacker's reward increases from $1$ to $2.9$.

\section{Model}
Let $\G=(V,E)$ be a reported social graph with seller $s\in V$ and buyers $N=V\setminus\{s\}$. The seller has $K\ge 1$ identical items. Each real buyer $i$ has single-unit demand and value $v_i\ge 0$ for receiving one item. Buyer $i$ reports a bid $v'_i$ and a set $r'_i\subseteq r_i$ of neighbors to whom the information is forwarded.

\begin{definition}[Non-colluding type-preserving split attack]
A real buyer $i$ may replace itself by a finite set $\Sigma_i$ of fake identities, all controlled by $i$. These fake identities inherit the same underlying valuation as the creator and may only redistribute the creator's incident edges inside the creator's dominated region. Distinct real buyers do not collude.
\end{definition}

The real-buyer utility is aggregated over the creator and all its fake identities. Since demand is unit,
\begin{equation}
\util_i(x,p)=v_i\,\ind[\exists t\in \{i\}\cup \Sigma_i : x_t=1]-\sum_{t\in \{i\}\cup \Sigma_i}p_t .
\label{eq:realu}
\end{equation}

We will use dominators in the reported graph rooted at the seller. If every path from $s$ to $j$ passes through $i$, then $i$ dominates $j$. The immediate dominator of $j$ is written $\idom(j)$.

\section{Formal Algorithmic Description of SC-GIDM}
Our mechanism combines dominator clustering with a cluster-level GIDM run.

\begin{framed}
\noindent\textbf{Sybil-Cluster Generalized Information Diffusion Mechanism (SC-GIDM)}\\
\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
Given the reported type profile $\theta'=(G(\theta'),(v'_i)_{i\in N})$ and $K$ homogeneous items, let $s$ denote the seller.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Compute the dominator tree $T_{\mathrm{dom}}$ of $G(\theta')$ rooted at $s$. For each node $i$ with $\mathrm{idom}(i)=s$, define one cluster
  \[
  V_i=\{j\in N \mid i \text{ dominates } j\}\cup\{i\}.
  \]
  Let $\mathcal{C}=\{V_1,\dots,V_m\}$ be the resulting disjoint cluster family.

  \item Build the macro graph $G_{\mathrm{cluster}}=(\mathcal{C},E_{\mathrm{cluster}})$ where
  \[
  (V_i,V_j)\in E_{\mathrm{cluster}}
  \iff
  \exists u\in V_i,\ \exists v\in V_j \text{ with } (u,v)\in E(G(\theta'))
  \]
  For each cluster $V_i$, define a representative
  \[
  \eta(V_i)\in\argmax_{j\in V_i} v'_j
  \]
  using a fixed tie-breaking rule, and set
  \[
  \widehat b_{V_i}=v'_{\eta(V_i)},\qquad \mathrm{cap}(V_i)=1.
  \]

  \item Similar to SCM \cite{ChenDWWZ23}, randomly sample a spanning tree of $G_{\mathrm{cluster}}$, call it $T_H$, and delete the remaining inter-cluster edges. This pruning step may turn more clusters into critical clusters and therefore create positive intermediary rewards. 

  \item Run GIDM on the macro instance
  \[
  \bigl(T_H,(\widehat b_{V_i})_{V_i\in\mathcal{C}},K\bigr).
  \]
  Let $W^*(\theta')\subseteq\mathcal{C}$ be the winning-cluster set and define
  \[
  I^*(\theta')=\bigcup_{V_j\in W^*(\theta')} P_{V_j}(\theta').
  \]
  The GIDM allocation/payment rule used here is exactly the macro-level version of Appendix~\ref{app:gidm}.

  \item For each cluster $V_i\in\mathcal{C}$, define the cluster-level transfer
  \[
  x_{V_i}(\theta')=
  \begin{cases}
  SW^{\mathrm{macro}}_{-D_{V_i}}-\Bigl(SW^{\mathrm{macro}}_{-C^K_{V_i}}-\widehat b_{V_i}\Bigr),
  & \text{if } V_i\in W^*(\theta'),\\[1ex]
  SW^{\mathrm{macro}}_{-D_{V_i}}-SW^{\mathrm{macro}}_{-C^K_{V_i}},
  & \text{if } V_i\in I^*(\theta')\setminus  W^*(\theta')\\[1ex]
  0,
  & \text{otherwise,}
  \end{cases}
  \]
  where
  \[
  D_{V_i}(\theta')=\{V_i\}\cup C_{V_i}(\theta').
  \]

  \item Lift the macro outcome to identities. The allocation rule is
  \[
  \pi^{\mathrm{scg}}_j(\theta')=
  \begin{cases}
  1,
  & \text{if } j=\eta(V_i) \text{ for some } V_i\in W^*(\theta'),\\
  0,
  & \text{otherwise,}
  \end{cases}
  \]
  so the highest-bid identity in each winning cluster gets the item. The payment rule is
  \[
  p^{\mathrm{scg}}_j(\theta')=
  \begin{cases}
  x_{V_i}(\theta'),
  & \text{if } V_i\in W^*(\theta') \text{ and } j=\eta(V_i),\\
  x_{V_i}(\theta'),
  & \text{if } V_i\in I^*(\theta')\setminus W^*(\theta') \text{ and } j=\rho(V_i),\\
  0,
  & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{framed}

\begin{remark}
The item and the intermediary reward are separated inside a cluster. If the cluster wins, the physical item goes to the highest bidder $\eta(V_i)$. If the cluster is merely intermediary, the cluster-root $\rho(V_i)$ receives the reward. Internal Sybil nodes do not receive separate intermediary rewards.
\end{remark}

\section{Main Results}
We first show that a buyer's split identities remain confined inside a single cluster.

\begin{lemma}[Cluster containment]
\label{lem:contain}
Fix a real buyer $i$ and a non-colluding type-preserving split attack by $i$. Then every fake identity created by $i$ belongs to the same dominator cluster as $i$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Let $V_r$ be the cluster containing $i$, where $\idom(r)=s$ and every path from $s$ to $i$ passes through $r$. By the attack model, the fake identities of $i$ can only redistribute $i$'s incident edges inside the dominated region already reachable through $i$; they cannot create a new incoming path from outside that region. Hence every path from $s$ to any fake identity must still pass through $r$. Therefore every fake identity created by $i$ is also dominated by $r$, so it belongs to the same cluster $V_r$.
\end{proof}

The next lemma shows that, after clustering, the macro instance is unchanged by a split attack.
\begin{lemma}[Macro-instance invariance]
\label{lem:macro}
Under the attack model of Section~4, a split attack by one buyer does not change the macro nodes, the macro adjacency structure, or the macro bid vector used by SC-GIDM.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:contain}, all fake identities of the attacker lie in the same cluster as the attacker, so the set of macro nodes is unchanged. Since fake identities cannot create new external paths outside the attacker's dominated region, they do not create new inter-cluster adjacency. Thus the cluster graph and any spanning tree sampled from it are unchanged. Finally, the attack is type-preserving, so all fake identities have the same underlying valuation as the attacker. Hence the cluster representative value $\widehat b_{V_i}=\max_{j\in V_i} v'_j$ is unchanged for the attacker's cluster, and of course unchanged for all other clusters.
\end{proof}

We can now prove the main robustness theorem.
\begin{theorem}[SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof]
\label{thm:sybil}
Under the non-colluding type-preserving split-attack model, SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof: a buyer cannot improve her real-buyer utility in~\eqref{eq:realu} by replacing herself with finitely many fake identities.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Fix a real buyer $i$ and any split attack by $i$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:macro}, the macro instance seen by SC-GIDM is identical before and after the attack: the cluster tree $T_H$, macro bids, critical sets, winner set, and cluster-level transfers are all the same. Therefore the macro GIDM allocation and payment are unchanged.

Now compare the real-buyer utility of $i$ before and after the split attack. Since the macro instance is unchanged, either the attacker's cluster wins in both instances or it loses in both. If it loses, the aggregated utility is the same because every payment inside the cluster is the same and the buyer gets no item in either case. If it wins, the cluster-level payment remains the same and the value contributed by receiving one item remains $v_i$ because demand is unit. The item may be assigned to the attacker itself or to one of its fake identities, but the utility in~\eqref{eq:realu} aggregates over the entire set $\{i\}\cup\Sigma_i$. Hence the total utility is unchanged.

Therefore no split attack can strictly improve the buyer's real-buyer utility. SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof.
\end{proof}

The next theorem states what SC-GIDM inherits from GIDM.
\begin{theorem}[Inheritance of GIDM properties]
\label{thm:inherit}
Fix any reported profile and the corresponding sampled cluster tree $T_H$. On this macro instance, SC-GIDM inherits from GIDM the following properties:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item feasibility of allocation,
    \item individual rationality,
    \item incentive compatibility at the macro level,
    \item weak budget balance.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
After clustering and pruning, SC-GIDM simply runs GIDM on the macro instance $(T_H,(\widehat b_{V_i})_{V_i\in\Ccal},K)$ and then lifts the macro outcome back to identities. Hence every macro winner and every macro intermediary is treated exactly according to GIDM's allocation/payment rule. The four listed properties are therefore immediate consequences of the corresponding GIDM guarantees proved in \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Possible wastefulness]
SC-GIDM can be wasteful if the clustering and tree-pruning steps leave fewer than $K$ effective clusters that can receive items. In that case the mechanism still remains feasible and weakly budget balanced, but not all $K$ items need be allocated. Thus the inheritance theorem is about GIDM's strategic and budgetary properties on the macro instance, not about full non-wastefulness on the original identity graph.
\end{remark}

We finally justify the internal reward rule.
\begin{theorem}[Weak incentive compatibility of intra-cluster reward distribution]
\label{thm:weakreward}
Consider a cluster $V_i$ that is intermediary on the sampled cluster tree. Paying the intermediary reward $x_{V_i}$ to the cluster root $\rho(V_i)$ is weakly incentive compatible with respect to participation and internal role uncertainty.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
First, the amount $x_{V_i}$ depends only on the macro critical role of $V_i$ in the cluster tree. Internal rearrangements of nodes inside $V_i$ cannot create a second reward-bearing macro position. Hence a non-root node cannot increase the cluster-level intermediary reward by local manipulation.

Second, before the full diffusion process and cluster construction are realized, an identity does not know whether it will end up outside all critical clusters, become the cluster representative $\eta(V_i)$ of a winning cluster, or be the root $\rho(V_i)$ of an intermediary cluster. Abstaining from participation yields utility $0$. By Theorem~\ref{thm:inherit}, participation in the macro GIDM instance yields non-negative utility whenever the cluster wins or becomes reward-bearing. Therefore participation weakly dominates abstention ex ante.

Thus assigning intermediary rewards to cluster roots is weakly incentive compatible: internal identities cannot create more intermediary reward for themselves by local manipulation, and participation preserves a weakly better ex ante utility than abstention.
\end{proof}

\section{Conclusions}
The three baseline papers suggest a natural progression: IDM explains truthful diffusion for one item, GIDM extends it to multiple homogeneous items, and SCM shows that false identities fundamentally matter. Our report contributes a bridge between the latter two directions. We formalize SC-GIDM, explain why ordinary GIDM is Sybil-vulnerable, and prove that under a non-colluding type-preserving split model the macro instance built by SC-GIDM is invariant to a split attack. This yields a clean Sybil-proofness theorem and an inheritance theorem from GIDM. The main limitation, and therefore the clearest future work, is to go beyond this restricted attack model and to analyze what happens when fake identities can alter aggregate cluster bids or when multiple real buyers collude.

\section{Appendix: GIDM Pseudocode}
\label{app:gidm}
The following is the macro-level GIDM routine used inside SC-GIDM, adapted directly from Zhao et al.\ \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}. We include it here because SC-GIDM in Section~5 invokes GIDM as a subroutine.

\begin{framed}
\noindent\textbf{Generalized Information Diffusion Mechanism (GIDM)}\\
\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
Given the buyers' type profile $\theta$ and their action $\theta^\prime\in \mathcal{F}(\theta)$, compute the optimal allocation tree $T^{opt}(\theta^\prime)$. Let $W$ be the set of buyers who receive an item in GIDM, initially $W=\emptyset$. For each $i\in W$, define $GetFrom(i)$ to be the buyer from whom the item was taken by $i$ from the efficient allocation.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Allocation:} Let $Q$ be a last-in-first-out stack. The seller gives $w_i(T^{opt}(\theta^\prime))$ items to each $i\in Children(s)$ and adds all $Children(s)$ into $Q$. Repeatedly perform the allocation process:
\begin{framed}
\noindent\textbf{The Allocation of GIDM}\\
\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item Remove a node $i$ from $Q$, and add $i$ to $W$ if $i$ receives an item in the feasible allocation maximizing $\SW_{-\mathcal{C}_i^{K}}$ subject to the GIDM critical-child constraints.
\item If $i\in W$:
\begin{itemize}
\item if $\sum_{j\in Children(i)} w_j(T^{opt}(\theta^\prime)) = w_i(T^{opt}(\theta^\prime)) - 1$, set $GetFrom(i)=i$;
\item otherwise, let $k_i=w_i(T^{opt}(\theta^\prime))$, let $out$ be the buyer with the $k_i$-th largest reported value in the subtree rooted at $i$ and $w_{out}(T^{opt}(\theta^\prime))\neq 0$, decrease the corresponding subtree counts by one along the path to $out$, and set $GetFrom(i)=out$.
\end{itemize}
\item For each child $j$ of $i$, if $w_j(T^{opt}(\theta^\prime))>0$, give $w_j(T^{opt}(\theta^\prime))$ items to $j$ and add $j$ into $Q$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{framed}

\item \textbf{Payment:} For all $i$,
\[
\begin{cases}
\SW_{-D_i} - (\SW_{-\mathcal{C}_i^{K}} - v_i^\prime) & \text{if } i\in W,\\
\SW_{-D_i} - \SW_{-\mathcal{C}_i^{K}} & \text{if } i\in \bigcup_{j\in W}\mathcal{P}_j(\theta^\prime)\setminus W,\\
0 & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\]
\end{itemize}
\end{framed}

\section{Appendix: SCM Pseudocode}
\label{app:scm}
Our spanning-tree step in SC-GIDM is inspired by SCM \cite{ChenDWWZ23}, which prunes redundant inter-cluster edges before running a truthful single-item mechanism.\\
% \begin{framed}
\noindent\textbf{Sybil Cluster Mechanism (SCM) :}\\
\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
\item Given the reported type profile $\theta'$ as input, reconstruct a social graph
\[
H=(\Gamma(\theta'),E(H)),
\]
where
\[
E(H)=\{(x,y)\mid \exists i\in K_x,\ \exists j\in K_y \text{ such that } (i,j)\in E(G_s(\theta'))\}.
\]
\item Sample a random shortest-path tree of $H$ with equal probability, denoted by $T_H$.
\item Construct a subgraph $\widehat G$ of $G_s(\theta')$ using $T_H$ by deleting every edge
\[
(i,j)\quad \text{with } i\in K_x,\ j\in K_y,\ x\neq y,\ (x,y)\notin E(T_H).
\]
\item Run STM on the modified input $(\widehat G,\Gamma(\theta'))$ instead of on the original graph.
\end{enumerate}
