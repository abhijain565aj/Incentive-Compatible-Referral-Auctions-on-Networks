\begin{abstract}
Diffusion auctions sell items through a social network: buyers not only bid, but also decide whether to forward the auction information. Li et al. introduced the single-item diffusion setting and showed that the information diffusion mechanism (IDM) is individually rational and non-deficit, while Zhao et al. extended the model to multiple homogeneous items through GIDM. Chen et al. later proved that diffusion rewards are vulnerable to Sybil attacks and proposed Sybil-proof mechanisms only for the single-item case. We study the open multi-item direction. Our mechanism, \emph{SC-GIDM}, first clusters the reported graph by dominator structure, constructs a cluster graph, and then runs GIDM on this macro instance. We prove two results. First, under a non-colluding split-attack model, every fake identity created by a buyer remains inside that buyer's original dominator cluster, so splitting cannot manipulate the macro graph; consequently SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof. Second, since SC-GIDM runs GIDM on the cluster graph, it inherits the corresponding macro-level properties of GIDM, including individual rationality, incentive compatibility, and weak budget balance. We also identify the precise sense in which SC-GIDM can be wasteful: if fewer than $K$ clusters are formed, the unit-capacity restriction at the cluster level may leave some items unallocated.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{diffusion auction, social network, Sybil attack, multi-item mechanism, mechanism design}

\section{Introduction}
Classical auction theory assumes that the seller can directly contact all relevant participants. In social-network markets this assumption is unrealistic. A seller can usually inform only a small neighborhood, and more distant buyers learn about the auction only when already informed agents choose to propagate the information. This creates the central tension in diffusion auctions: a buyer can help the seller reach new participants, but may simultaneously dislike inviting stronger competitors.

This setting is not merely a theoretical curiosity. Referral-based commerce, community-driven resale platforms, creator-led product launches, and social coupon campaigns all rely on network propagation. In such applications, the auctioneer does not observe the full market directly and must instead incentivize information flow through strategic buyers. A mechanism that ignores the propagation stage can miss high-value buyers entirely. A mechanism that rewards propagation too aggressively, however, may pay too much to intermediaries or become vulnerable to manipulation.

Li et al. formalized this setting for one item and showed that the information diffusion mechanism (IDM) is individually rational (IR), incentive compatible (IC), and weakly budget balanced, whereas a direct network-VCG extension may create deficit \cite{LiHZZ17}. Zhao et al. then extended the framework to $K$ homogeneous items with single-unit-demand buyers via GIDM \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}. A second difficulty is robustness. Chen et al. showed that graph-dependent rewards make diffusion mechanisms vulnerable to \emph{Sybil attacks}, in which a real buyer creates fake identities to alter the critical positions used for pricing and rewards \cite{ChenDWWZ23}. Their paper develops the single-item mechanisms STM and SCM and explicitly leaves the multi-item case as future work.

This report studies that open direction. Our goal is deliberately narrow. We do not attempt a complete false-name-proof solution for arbitrary combinatorial settings. Instead, we focus on the setting suggested by the baseline papers: the seller holds $K\ge 1$ homogeneous items, every physical buyer is unit demand, and Sybil attacks are non-colluding split attacks. In this setting we propose \emph{SC-GIDM} (Sybil-Cluster GIDM), a mechanism that first compresses the reported graph into dominator clusters and then runs GIDM on the resulting macro graph. The clustering step is meant to isolate the exact graph feature that Sybil attacks exploit: the ability to manufacture new graph positions that appear to be independent intermediaries.

\subsection{Contributions}
Our report makes three contributions.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item We formalize SC-GIDM as a dominator-cluster reduction followed by GIDM on the cluster graph.
    \item We prove that SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof under non-colluding split attacks: fake identities created by a buyer remain inside a single pre-existing cluster, and hence cannot manipulate the cluster graph on which GIDM is executed.
    \item We prove that SC-GIDM inherits the macro-level guarantees of GIDM. In particular, it inherits IR, IC, and weak budget balance on the cluster graph. We also state precisely when SC-GIDM may be wasteful.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Related Work}
The classical efficient-auction backbone comes from Vickrey, Clarke, and Groves \cite{Vickrey61,Clarke71,Groves73}. Li et al. imported these ideas into the diffusion setting and identified the special role of \emph{diffusion criticality}: intermediaries on all information paths can become essential to reaching the highest bidder \cite{LiHZZ17}. Their key contribution was IDM, which rewards only price-improving diffusion and thereby restores non-deficit. Zhao et al. generalized this logic to the sale of multiple homogeneous items with unit-demand buyers via GIDM \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}. GIDM is not a repeated single-item procedure; it uses an optimal allocation tree and counterfactual welfare terms to define the winners and payments. Finally, Chen et al. showed that ordinary diffusion mechanisms are not false-name-proof: fake identities can be used to inflate graph-based rewards without creating real value \cite{ChenDWWZ23}. Their single-item mechanisms STM and SCM use graph structure to restrict such manipulation. Our report sits at the intersection of the latter two papers.

\section{Model}
Let $\G=(V,E)$ be a reported directed social graph with seller $s\in V$ and buyers $N=V\setminus\{s\}$. The seller has $K\ge 1$ homogeneous items. Each physical buyer is unit demand and has value $v_i\ge 0$ for receiving one item. As in the baseline diffusion model, an informed buyer reports both a valuation and a forwarding set, namely $(v'_i,r'_i)$ with $r'_i\subseteq r_i$ \cite{LiHZZ17}. Buyers who never receive the auction information are absent from the realized profile.

The desiderata are the standard ones from the diffusion-auction literature.
\begin{definition}[Incentive compatibility]
A mechanism is IC if truthful bidding and forwarding to all neighbors is a dominant strategy.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Individual rationality]
A mechanism is IR if every truthful participant gets nonnegative utility.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Weak budget balance]
A mechanism is weakly budget balanced if the seller revenue is nonnegative on every feasible profile.
\end{definition}

We consider the following attack model.
\begin{definition}[Non-colluding split attack]
A split attack by buyer $x$ replaces $x$ by a finite set of fake identities controlled only by $x$. Every incoming edge of a fake identity must originate from $x$ or from another fake identity created by $x$; in particular, honest external buyers do not create new incoming edges toward the fake identities. Outgoing edges of $x$ may be redistributed among $x$ and its fake identities, but the attack does not create new honest neighbors. Distinct real buyers do not collude.
\end{definition}

The dominator relation is computed in the reported graph rooted at $s$. For a buyer $u$, let $\idom(u)$ denote its immediate dominator. For each node $i$ satisfying $\idom(i)=s$, define the cluster
\[
V_i=\{j\in N\mid i \text{ dominates } j\}\cup\{i\}.
\]
The family of such sets is denoted by $\Ccal=\{V_1,\dots,V_m\}$. Since children of the seller in a dominator tree partition all reachable descendants, these clusters form a disjoint partition of the reachable buyers.

\section{Why Clustering Helps: A Running Example}
Before presenting the formal mechanism, it is useful to see the role of clustering on a small example. Suppose the seller $s$ reaches two primary branches through buyers $a$ and $b$. Buyer $a$ dominates a high-value subtree containing $c$, while buyer $b$ dominates a different subtree containing $d$. In the ordinary identity-level graph, buyer $b$ may create fake identities below itself and attempt to appear as multiple intermediaries. If the mechanism directly rewards graph positions, these new identities can distort the apparent structure of the diffusion process.

SC-GIDM does not evaluate the attacked identity graph directly. It first asks which nodes are dominated by each seller-child. In the example of Figure~\ref{fig:cluster}, every fake identity created by $b$ remains in the same dominator region as $b$ and is therefore absorbed into the same cluster $V_b$. Thus the attacked micro-graph is reduced to the same two macro-nodes $V_a$ and $V_b$. Whatever happens internally inside $V_b$ no longer changes the macro graph. This is precisely the point where our approach differs from ordinary diffusion mechanisms: we do not try to price the attacked micro-structure; we remove that micro-structure before running GIDM.

A second example clarifies the conservative nature of the macro bid rule. Suppose $K=1$, cluster $V_a$ has root bid $9$, cluster $V_b$ has root bid $7$, and a fake child inside $V_b$ reports $100$. Since SC-GIDM uses the cluster root bid $\widehat b_{V_b}=v'_{\rhoC(V_b)}=7$, the fake internal bid does not change the macro instance at all. Consequently, if GIDM would choose $V_a$ on the truthful macro graph, it still chooses $V_a$ after the split attack. This is the main robustness tradeoff embodied in the mechanism: the cluster abstraction discards some internal information, but in exchange it makes internal false-name manipulation strategically irrelevant.

\section{Formal Algorithmic Description of SC-GIDM}
\begin{framed}
\noindent\textbf{Sybil-Cluster Generalized Information Diffusion Mechanism (SC-GIDM)}\\
\rule{\linewidth}{0.5pt}
Given the reported type profile $\theta'=(G(\theta'),(v'_i)_{i\in N})$ and $K$ homogeneous items, let $s$ denote the seller.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Compute the dominator tree $T_{\mathrm{dom}}$ of $G(\theta')$ rooted at $s$. For each node $i$ with $\idom(i)=s$, define one cluster
  \[
  V_i=\{j\in N \mid i \text{ dominates } j\}\cup\{i\}.
  \]
  Let $\Ccal=\{V_1,\dots,V_m\}$ be the resulting disjoint cluster family.

  \item Build the macro graph $\QG=(\Ccal,E_{\mathrm{cluster}})$ where
  \[
  (V_i,V_j)\in E_{\mathrm{cluster}}
  \iff
  \exists u\in V_i,\ \exists v\in V_j \text{ with } (u,v)\in E(G(\theta')).
  \]
  For each cluster $V_i$, define the macro bid and the macro capacity as
  \[
  \widehat b_{V_i}=v'_{\rhoC(V_i)},\qquad \mathrm{cap}(V_i)=1,
  \]
  where $\rhoC(V_i)=i$ is the cluster root.

  \item Run GIDM on the macro instance
  \[
  \bigl(\QG,(\widehat b_{V_i})_{V_i\in\Ccal},K\bigr).
  \]
  Let $W^*(\theta')\subseteq\Ccal$ be the winning-cluster set and define
  \[
  I^*(\theta')=\bigcup_{V_j\in W^*(\theta')} P_{V_j}(\theta').
  \]

  \item For each cluster $V_i\in\Ccal$, define the cluster-level transfer
  \[
  x_{V_i}(\theta')=
  \begin{cases}
  SW^{\mathrm{macro}}_{-D_{V_i}}-\Bigl(SW^{\mathrm{macro}}_{-C^K_{V_i}}-\widehat b_{V_i}\Bigr),
  & \text{if } V_i\in W^*(\theta'),\\[1ex]
  SW^{\mathrm{macro}}_{-D_{V_i}}-SW^{\mathrm{macro}}_{-C^K_{V_i}},
  & \text{if } V_i\in I^*(\theta')\setminus W^*(\theta'),\\[1ex]
  0,
  & \text{otherwise,}
  \end{cases}
  \]
  where
  \[
  D_{V_i}(\theta')=\{V_i\}\cup C_{V_i}(\theta'),
  \]
  and $C^K_{V_i}(\theta')$ is the macro critical set induced by GIDM.

  \item Lift the macro outcome to identities. The allocation rule is
  \[
  \pi^{\mathrm{scg}}_j(\theta')=
  \begin{cases}
  1,
  & \text{if } j=\rhoC(V_i) \text{ for some } V_i\in W^*(\theta'),\\
  0,
  & \text{otherwise,}
  \end{cases}
  \]
  and the payment rule is
  \[
  p^{\mathrm{scg}}_j(\theta')=
  \begin{cases}
  x_{V_i}(\theta'),
  & \text{if } j=\rhoC(V_i) \text{ for some } V_i\in\Ccal,\\
  0,
  & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{framed}

The mechanism therefore has a clear division of labor: all price and reward computation happens at the cluster level, and all monetary transfer is settled only through the cluster root. Internal fake identities do not receive independent reward positions. The use of the root bid is conservative but deliberate: it ensures that a buyer cannot improve the macro instance merely by creating a fake high-bid descendant.

\section{Main Results}
We begin with the structural fact behind the Sybil-proofness argument.
\begin{lemma}[Sybils remain inside one pre-existing cluster]
\label{lem:containment}
Let $x$ be a real buyer and let $V_i\in\Ccal$ be the unique cluster containing $x$. Under any non-colluding split attack by $x$, every fake identity created by $x$ also lies in $V_i$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Fix any fake identity $y$ created by $x$. By the attack model, every incoming edge to $y$ originates either from $x$ or from another fake identity created by $x$. Hence every path from the seller $s$ to $y$ must pass through $x$. Therefore $x$ dominates $y$. Since $x\in V_i$, the root $i$ of that cluster dominates $x$, and dominance is transitive. Thus $i$ dominates $y$. By the definition of $V_i$, every node dominated by $i$ belongs to $V_i$. Hence $y\in V_i$.
\end{proof}

The next lemma explains why splitting cannot alter the macro instance seen by GIDM.
\begin{lemma}[Split attacks do not create new macro nodes or new rewarded positions]
\label{lem:macro}
Fix a real buyer $x$ and let $V_i$ be the cluster containing $x$. Under any non-colluding split attack by $x$, the family of clusters outside $V_i$ is unchanged, no new cluster is created, and every monetary transfer generated by SC-GIDM remains attached to the same set of cluster roots as before. The only effect of the attack is an internal modification of $V_i$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:containment}, every fake identity created by $x$ remains inside $V_i$. Therefore no new cluster appears in the partition $\Ccal$, and every cluster different from $V_i$ keeps exactly the same vertex set as before. Since the attack does not create new honest neighbors, the set of external clusters adjacent to $V_i$ is also unchanged: the attacker may redistribute outgoing edges among $x$ and its fake identities, but all those outgoing edges still target the same pre-existing honest neighborhoods. Hence the macro graph $\QG$ does not gain new vertices or new macro adjacency patterns due to the split attack.

Since SC-GIDM computes rewards and payments only after building the macro graph, and these transfers are defined only for clusters and paid only to cluster roots, fake identities do not become new payment-receiving positions. In particular, splitting $x$ changes only the internal structure of the already existing cluster $V_i$.
\end{proof}

We can now prove the main Sybil-proofness theorem.
\begin{theorem}[SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof under non-colluding split attacks]
\label{thm:sybil}
Under the attack model of Section~3, a buyer cannot increase her utility by replacing herself with finitely many fake identities. Hence SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Fix a real buyer $x$ and let $V_i$ be the cluster containing $x$. Consider any non-colluding split attack by $x$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:containment}, all fake identities created by $x$ remain inside $V_i$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:macro}, the attack creates no new cluster and no new rewarded macro position. Hence the attacker cannot manipulate the cluster graph on which GIDM is run.

It follows that, from the viewpoint of the macro mechanism, the split attack does not create a new strategic player: the buyer $x$ and all her fake identities act only through the already existing cluster $V_i$. Moreover, because SC-GIDM uses the root bid $\widehat b_{V_i}=v'_{\rhoC(V_i)}$, fake descendants cannot alter the macro bid by overbidding internally. Therefore the entire effect of the split attack is equivalent to an internal re-expression of the same cluster-level participant already present in the macro instance.

Since SC-GIDM runs GIDM on the cluster graph, the relevant strategic choices reduce to the cluster-level report of $V_i$ on that graph. GIDM is incentive compatible, so truthful bidding and forwarding to all neighbors is a dominant strategy on the macro instance \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}. Therefore the split attack cannot generate strictly higher utility than the truthful cluster-level behavior.

Finally, SC-GIDM settles payments and rewards only at cluster roots. Internal fake identities receive no direct reward and do not become independent recipients of intermediary transfers. Thus splitting cannot create extra payment or reward channels inside $V_i$. Consequently, buyer $x$ cannot improve her utility through a non-colluding split attack. Hence SC-GIDM is Sybil-proof.
\end{proof}

The second theorem states the inherited guarantees.
\begin{theorem}[SC-GIDM inherits the macro-level properties of GIDM]
\label{thm:inherit}
When executed on the cluster graph, SC-GIDM inherits the corresponding properties of GIDM. In particular, at the macro-cluster level, SC-GIDM is individually rational, incentive compatible, and weakly budget balanced.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By definition, SC-GIDM first constructs the cluster graph $\QG$ and then runs GIDM on the macro instance $\bigl(\QG,(\widehat b_{V_i})_{V_i\in\Ccal},K\bigr)$. The winning cluster set, the intermediary cluster set, and the transfers $x_{V_i}(\theta')$ are exactly the corresponding GIDM outputs on that macro instance. Therefore every property that GIDM guarantees for its input graph also holds for SC-GIDM on the cluster graph.

Zhao et al. prove that GIDM is IR, IC, and weakly budget balanced for homogeneous items and unit-demand buyers \cite{ZhaoLXHJ18}. Applying those guarantees to the macro instance yields the conclusion. Since SC-GIDM merely lifts the macro outcome back to the cluster roots, the seller revenue of SC-GIDM is exactly the seller revenue of GIDM on the cluster graph.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[On wastefulness]
SC-GIDM is potentially wasteful only because every cluster has capacity one. If fewer than $K$ clusters are formed, then at most that many items can be allocated, so some items may remain unassigned. If at least $K$ reachable clusters exist, then the mechanism is non-wasteful at the macro level: the cluster graph contains enough unit-capacity macro bidders to absorb all $K$ items. More generally, the mechanism trades some internal flexibility for robustness: once a large attacked region is compressed into one macro bidder, the seller cannot allocate multiple items inside that region even if several internal nodes are present.
\end{remark}

\section{Conclusions}
The baseline papers suggest a natural path: IDM explains truthful diffusion for one item, GIDM extends this logic to multiple homogeneous items, and STM/SCM show that Sybil attacks fundamentally matter in graph-based reward mechanisms. Our report provides a restricted but mathematically explicit bridge between these directions. SC-GIDM first compresses the reported graph into dominator clusters and then executes GIDM on the resulting macro graph. The first main theorem shows that non-colluding split attacks cannot alter the macro graph because fake identities remain trapped inside an already existing cluster. The second theorem shows that SC-GIDM inherits the macro-level guarantees of GIDM.

The main open issue is therefore no longer the cluster-level mechanism, but the clustering layer itself: obtaining robust cluster formation without assuming the particular attack structure used here remains an important next step. A second natural direction is to relax the conservative root-bid abstraction while preserving Sybil-proofness. This would require a more delicate argument about what information from within a cluster can safely influence the macro instance.
